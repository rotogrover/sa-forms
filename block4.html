Explanation & Educational Objective

A cross-sectional study seeks to identify the prevalence of a condition at a particular point in time. In this scenario, these studies examine the use of tanning beds and the development of melanoma at a single cross-section of time. Thus, the risk factor (tanning beds) and the outcomes (melanoma) are measured simultaneously. Cross- sectional studies do not follow patients over time. All information is collected at a single point, so causation cannot be determined because the temporal sequence of events cannot be examined. In these studies, the development of melanoma may have preceded the use of tanning beds. Cross-sectional studies can, however, determine correlation. Correlation relates the trends of two variables together and is quantified by the Pearson correlation coefficient (r). It represents the increase, decrease, or absence, or absence of change in one variable with changes in the other. In cases where variables trend together (both increase), the r value is positive. The closer the value of the correlation coefficient to 1.0, the stronger the trend between the two variables. Negative r values suggest that data trend in opposing fashion; as one variable increases, the other decreases. Uncorrelated data will have a correlation coefficient near zero, which indicates that changes in one variable are not associated with any trend in the other variable. Explanation & Educational Objective

A Type II error occurs when a study shows no difference between the null and alternative hypotheses, when in fact, a difference exists. It is known as a "false negative· error and can be reduced by increasing the power of the study through a larger sample size and increasing precision in measurements. The Type II error rate is calculated by subtracting the power from 100%. A Type II error threshold is set at the beta level. Power, or the likelihood that the investigators will find the difference if it is there. is equal to one minus the beta level. In contrast, a Type I error occurs when the investigator shows a difference that does not actually exist. It is a false-positive error. with the error threshold set at the alpha level. This is indicative of a study that was able to accurately identify a true difference. Choice B is a Type I error. The investigators in this scenario inaccurately rejected the null hypothesis of no difference between the groups, accepting the alternative hypothesis that suggested the intervention was associated with a decreased incidence in hypertension. This difference identified does not actually exist. Choice D shows an appropriate acceptance of the null hypothesis. In this example. there are no significant differences in the intervention arm, which was appropriately identified by the investigators.

B1 Q1

A 29-year-old woman develops headache and shortness of breath 10 minutes after a transfusion of packed red blood cells is begun. Her temperature is 37.8°C (100°F), pulse is 100/min, respirations are 20/min, and blood pressure is 90/60 mm Hg. The transfusion is stopped immediately. Laboratory studies show free hemoglobin in urine and plasma. Which of the following erythrocyte blood group antigens is the most likely cause of this patient's transfusion reaction?

A.ABO
B.Duffy
C.Kell
D.MN
E.Rh
Correct Answer: A
Explanation & Educational Objective

Acute hemolytic transfusion reactions (AHTR) most often occur due to ABO incompatibility, which occurs when a patient receives an inappropriately typed and crossed transfusion product. When the patient receives an ABO incompatible blood product, preformed antibodies bind to antigens on the donor red blood cells. This leads to acute hemolysis as the patient's antibodies attack the transfused red blood cells (type II hypersensitivity reaction). Symptoms typically begin within an hour of the start of transfusion, and patients may present with fever, flank pain, chills, hypotension, and dark urine. If AHTR is suspected, the transfusion should be stopped immediately. Treatment involves supportive care with aggressive fluid administration, hemodynamic support, and pain management. Incorrect Answers: B, C, D. and E. Duffy (Choice B) and Kell (Choice C) are both antigens found on the surface of red blood cells. Transfusion of Duffy or Kell incompatible blood products can lead to severe AHTR. but the more common cause is ABO incompatibility. MN (Choice D) antigens are located on glycophorin A, which is a glycoprotein that is the primary determinant of the net negative charge of the red blood cell membrane. Transfusion reactions due to MN incompatibility are rare. Rh (Choice E) incompatibility is the most common cause of hemolytic disease of the newborn (HDN). HDN occurs in patients who are Rh(D)-positive and are born to mothers who are Rh(D)-negative but who were previously sensitized to Rh antigen either by a prior birth or blood transfusion.

Educational Objective

Acute hemolytic transfusion reactions (AHTR) most often occur due to ABO incompatibility, which occurs when a patient receives an inappropriately typed and crossed transfusion product. Patients will present with fever, flank pain, chills, hypotension, and dark urine within an hour of the start of the transfusion.

B1 Q2

A 62-year-old man with ischemic heart disease is admitted to the hospital because of a 4-day history of increasingly severe chest pain. Physical examination shows no abnormalities. Angiography shows a blockage in the anterior inte,ventricular (left anterior descending) artery. A stent is placed to relieve the obstruction. The stent contains paclitaxel. This drug works to decrease the incidence of restenosis through which of the following mechanisms of action?

A.Enhanced endothelial growth over the stent
B.Increased adhesion of the stent to the atheromatous lesion
C.Prevention of platelet adhesion to the stent
D.Prevention of smooth muscle growth over the stent
E.Stabilization of the cholesterol core of the atheromatous lesion
Correct Answer: D
Explanation & Educational Objective

Neointima formation is a common cause of vascular restenosis after percutaneous coronary intervention for coronary artery disease. The neointima is primarily composed of vascular smooth muscle cells in a rich extracellular matrix that have migrated into the stent to form a layer that shares some features with the tunica intima. Migration is promoted by macrophages and inflammatory cells in the setting of a foreign body reaction to the stent. The resulting neointima formation decreases the lumen diameter and may result in inadequate myocardial perfusion during exertion with recurrence of angina. Drug-eluting stents attempt to reduce the risk for neointima formation by the continuous, slow release of drugs that inhibit cell proliferation and the prevention of smooth muscle growth over the stent. Incorrect Answers: A, B, C, and E. Enhanced endothelial growth over the stent (Choice A) is not associated with paclitaxel and other drug-eluting stent agents. An endothelial lining does eventually grow over the stent material. acting like a normal arterial vessel and reducing the risk for stent thrombosis over time. Increased adhesion of the stent to the atheromatous lesion (Choice B) is not associated with paclitaxel. Early stent adhesion around the lesion and growth of the endothelial lining are important in the functioning of a successful stent. Prevention of platelet adhesion to the stent (Choice C) is a vital component of therapy, and patients are typically prescribed multiple antiplatelet agents in the initial period after the stent is placed, typically aspirin and a thienopyridine. Paclitaxel is not an antiplatelet agent. Stabilization of the cholesterol core of the atheromatous lesion (Choice E) is typically achieved with statins. Drug-eluting stents contain anti motility agents such as paclitaxel (an antimicrotubule agent) to prevent neointima formation in the initial phase after stent placement.

Educational Objective

Neointima formation is a common cause of vascular restenosis after percutaneous coronary intervention. Drug-eluting stents reduce the risk for neointima formation through inhibition of smooth muscle cell proliferation and growth over the stent.

A 25-year-old man comes to the physician because of a 6-month history of diarrhea, fatigue, and weight loss. Physical examination shows pallor. Laboratory studies show macrocytic anemia, hypercellular bone marrow, and erythrocyte precursors that are abnormally large. Which of the following is the most likely explanation for the hematologic findings in this patient?

A.Defect in cytoskeleton of the erythrocyte membrane
B.Defect in a glycolytic enzyme of the erythrocyte
C.Hemoglobinopathy
D.Impaired DNA synthesis
E.Impaired heme synthesis
Correct Answer: D
Explanation & Educational Objective

Folic acid, or vitamin Bg, is converted in the body to tetrahydrofolic acid. Tetrahydrofolic acid helps form several coenzymes involved in purine and pyrimidine synthesis, nucleoprotein synthesis, and erythropoiesis. Impaired DNA synthesis can lead to ineffective hematopoiesis across cell lines, presenting as megaloblastic anemia. Megaloblastic anemia manifests pathologically as erythrocyte macrocytosis, hypersegmented neutrophils, and hypercellular bone marrow. Laboratory studies show anemia, increased mean corpuscular volume, and normal leukocyte and platelet indices. Folic acid is contained in leafy vegetables and primarily absorbed in the jejunum. Folic acid deficiency is often seen in patients with malnutrition, alcohol use disorder, and patients taking antifolate medications (eg, phenytoin, methotrexate). Patients with folic acid deficiency commonly report fatigue, diarrhea, and anorexia. Physical examination may show pallor. In the case of an otherwise healthy, young patient with chronic diarrhea, fatigue, and weight loss, diagnostic consideration should be paid to primary small intestinal pathology, especially malabsorption syndromes such as celiac disease. Incorrect Answers: A, B, C, and E. A defect in cytoskeleton of the erythrocyte membrane (Choice A) is found in hereditary spherocytosis. Hereditary spherocytosis is a hemolytic anemia that commonly presents with jaundice and splenomegaly. The cytoskeleton is intact in folic acid deficiency. A defect in a glycolytic enzyme of the erythrocyte (Choice B) is found in pyruvate kinase deficiency, a hemolytic anemia that commonly presents with jaundice and splenomegaly. Glycolysis is intact in folic acid deficiency. Hemoglobinopathy (Choice C) describes numerous conditions including thalassemia and sickle cell disease, among others. These conditions cause anemia due to deficient production of normal adult hemoglobin or production of abnormal hemoglobin. Reticulocytosis and increased bilirubin concentrations can be seen if hemolysis is present, but these conditions are chronic and present at birth. Impaired heme synthesis (Choice E) occurs in lead poisoning and porphyria, which manifest as microcytic anemia. Folic acid is not involved in heme synthesis.

Folic acid deficiency leads to impaired DNA synthesis, resulting in megaloblastic anemia. Cultures of fluid obtained from the two prior abscesses grew Staphylococcus aureus and Burkholderia cepacia. respectively. B.Chronic granulomatous disease
C.Job syndrome
D.Leukocyte adhesion deficiency
E.X-linked agammaglobulinemia
Correct Answer: B
Explanation & Educational Objective

Chronic granulomatous disease (CGD) is an immunodeficiency syndrome caused by a defect in the nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH) oxidase complex. which is essential for neutrophilic destruction of catalase-positive pathogens. Most defects are inherited in an X-linked pattern. NADPH oxidase uses oxygen as a substrate for the generation of free radicals (superoxide anions). Superoxide dismutase and myeloperoxidase subsequently use these free radical oxygen species for the creation of hydrogen peroxide and hypochlorous acid. This pathway, known as the respiratory burst. results in bacterial death. NADPH oxidase-deficient cells can use hydrogen peroxide from bacteria to generate a respiratory burst. but catalase-positive bacteria can neutralize their own hydrogen peroxide. thus leaving the host cells without the substrate necessary to complete the respiratory burst. Recurrent pneumonia is the most common presenting infection in patients with CGD, but pyogenic infections of the skin and soft tissue. lymph nodes, and liver are also encountered. Common infecting bacteria include Staphylococcus spp, B. cepacia. and Nocardia spp. Patients with CGD are at risk for fungal infections, especially Aspergillus spp. Diagnosis is made by an abnormal dihydrorhodamine test. or a nitroblue tetrazolium reduction test. In the latter test, normal phagocytes use the action of NADPH to reduce nitroblue, which leads to a color change from yellow to blue. Results from patients with CGD will not demonstrate color change. Incorrect Answers: A, C, D. and E. Chediak-Higashi syndrome (Choice A) is a rare. autosomal recessive disorder of the immune system caused by mutations in the lysosomal trafficking regulator gene (LYST) that encodes a protein essential for normal formation and transportation of lysosomes within the cell. Disruption of phagolysosome function leads to accumulation of giant granules in neutrophils, other granulocytes, and platelets. Clinical manifestations include recurrent bacterial infections, oculocutaneous albinism, peripheral neuropathy. and progressive neurologic dysfunction. Job syndrome (Choice C). also known as hyper-IgE syndrome, results from a defect in the JAK-STAT signaling pathway that leads to impaired Th17 cell differentiation. which in turn affects neutrophil chemotaxis. Patients present with coarse facies, eczema, and cutaneous cold abscesses, primarily from S. aureus. Leukocyte adhesion deficiency, or LAD (Choice D). is a group of disorders characterized by impaired leukocyte adhesion to the vascular endothelium in response to infection. Leukocytes retain the ability to phagocytose and eliminate foreign pathogens but are unable to migrate to sites of infection or inflammation in the extravascular space. LAD is typically characterized by recurrent bacterial infections. impaired wound healing, a delayed detachment of the umbilical cord after birth, and a lack of leukocytes at sites of infections with an absence of pus. X-linked (Bruton) agammaglobulinemia (Choice E) is caused by a mutation in the BTK gene, which encodes a tyrosine kinase essential for all stages of B lymphocyte development and proliferation. Patients with this gene mutation lack functioning B lymphocytes. resulting in low or absent serum immunoglobulins and recurrent bacterial infections caused by impaired humoral immunity. They do not typically exhibit susceptibility to viral or fungal infections, which are typically combated by T lymphocytes.

Chronic granulomatous disease (CGD) is caused by a mutation in the nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH) oxidase complex, which is necessary for the formation of reactive oxygen species in phagocytes. Deficiency results in impaired intracellular killing of pathogens by phagocytes. This presents as recurrent pyogenic infections with catalase-positive organisms such as S. aureus. B1 Q9

A 35-year-old man has a sinus tachycardia of 120/min. Which of the following best describes the relationship of ventricular contraction and relaxation in this patient?

A.Diastole and systole are equally prolonged
B.Diastole and systole are equally shortened
C.Diastole is prolonged more than systole
D.Diastole is shortened more than systole
E.Systole is prolonged more than diastole
F.Systole is shortened more than diastole
Correct Answer: D
Explanation & Educational Objective

Tachycardia results in significantly decreased diastolic filling time. In normal sinus rhythm, cardiac conduction starts with an electrical impulse in the sinoatrial (SA) node located in the upper right atrium. The electrical impulse is conducted throughout the atria, causing coordinated contraction of the right and left atria (atrial systole) with active filling of the ventricles. This is the ventricular diastolic phase. The electrical impulse reaches the atrioventricular (AV) node located in the interatrial septum. Conduction is temporarily slowed in the AV node to allow for complete active and passive filling of the ventricles (PR interval), followed by conduction of the electrical impulse down the His-Purkinje system in the interventricular septum, then to the right and left bundles, and finally to the myocardial cells of the ventricles. This results in contraction of the right and left ventricles (ventricular systole, QRS complex) with expulsion of blood into the pulmonary and systemic circulations in the systolic phase. The ventricles subsequently repolarize (T wave) and relax to allow filling for the next cardiac cycle. Filling of the ventricles is essential for maintaining cardiac output via the Frank-Starling mechanism. Incorrect Answers: A. B, C. E. and F. Diastole and systole are equally prolonged (Choice A) is incorrect. Tachycardia leads to a shortened diastolic phase, not a prolonged one. Similarly. diastole is prolonged more than systole (Choice C) is not correct either. Diastole and systole are equally shortened (Choice B) and systole is shortened more than diastole (Choice F) are incorrect. In a heart with normal conduction, the duration of systole depends on conduction time from the AV node to the distal ventricular conduction network, which is not significantly altered by tachycardia. Rather, the time between conduction of the initial impulse in the SA node is shortened, which corresponds with diastole. Systole is prolonged more than diastole (Choice E) does not occur in tachycardia, but is observed in conditions that impair ventricular conduction, such as bundle branch blocks. Increased afterload, such as with hypertension or aortic stenosis, also prolongs systole as ventricular contraction takes longer to complete.

Educational Objective

Tachycardia shortens the duration of diastole and ventricular filling time, reducing preload. Severe or prolonged tachycardia can result in inadequate cardiac output and heart failure.

A 58-year-old man with type 2 diabetes mellitus, a history of renal stones, dyslipidemia, and hypokalemia requires diuretic therapy for hypertension. Thiazide and loop diuretics differ most markedly in which of the following parameters of concern in this man?

A.Glucose tolerance
B.Plasma lipid concentrations
C.Plasma potassium concentration
D.Plasma uric acid concentration
E.Urinary calcium excretion
Correct Answer: E
Explanation & Educational Objective

The distal convoluted tubule (DCT) is the main site for Na• and er reabsorption in the nephron, making dilute and hypotonic filtrate. Hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ) and other thiazide diuretics (eg, chlorthalidone) inhibit NaCl reabsorption in the OCT by inhibiting the action of the sodium-chloride cotransporter (NCC). These drugs reduce the diluting capacity of the nephron and result in urinary losses of sodium and potassium. HCTZ has the opposite effect on calcium; calcium ion loss is reduced by thiazide diuretics, and they are occasionally referred to as "calcium sparing" diuretics. Thiazide diuretics are a first-line therapy for hypertension. In contrast, loop diuretics (eg, furosemide, torsemide, bumetanide, ethacrynic acid) act at the thick ascending limb of the loop of Henle to reduce solute (and therefore solvent) reabsorption. They inhibit the sodium-potassium-chloride cotransport channel and increase calcium excretion. making them also useful in the treatment of hypercalcemia. By contrast, loop diuretics, through promotion of calcium excretion, may worsen renal stones which are most frequently made of calcium. Thiazide diuretics may be used instead in such cases. Loop diuretics generally do not affect glycemic balance. Plasma lipid concentrations (Choice B) can be increased by both loop and thiazide diuretics. Plasma potassium concentration (Choice C) is decreased by both thiazide and loop diuretics, which both promote potassium excretion. Plasma uric acid concentration (Choice D) is increased by thiazide and loop diuretics.

Educational Objective

Both thiazide and loop diuretics reduce solute reabsorption. However, thiazide diuretics decrease calcium excretion, and loop diuretics increase calcium excretion, making thiazides more useful in cases of nephrolithiasis.

A 60-year-old woman is found to have osteoporosis. Her last menstrual period was 10 years ago. She has a history of deep venous thrombosis. B.Gonadotropins
C.Ovarian sex hormones
D.Selective estrogen receptor modulators
E.Vitamin D analogs
Correct Answer: A
Explanation & Educational Objective

Osteoporosis is caused by the loss of bone mineral density, commonly appearing in postmenopausal females of European descent and exacerbated in those with chronic steroid use and limited physical activity. The condition results from the interplay of three possible mechanisms: failure to achieve peak bone density. increased bone resorption. and decreased new bone formation. It is often asymptomatic until a minimally traumatic injury results in a fracture. most commonly involving the hip. distal radius. or vertebrae. Classically, serum calcium, parathyroid hormone (PTH). vitamin D. and phosphate are within normal limits. Decreased concentrations of vitamin D may contribute lo pathogenesis by causing increased concentrations of PTH even when serum calcium is within normal limits. The diagnosis of osteoporosis is made based on a DEXA scan once the bone density falls below 2.5 standard deviations of the mean for a young adult. Bisphosphonates are similar in structure to pyrophosphate, and thus, incorporate into the bone mineral matrix. When this bone mineral matrix is broken down by osteoclasts, bisphosphonates enter the osteoclasts and initiate osteoclast apoptosis.
